FLOWSPACE — BRAND VOICE GUIDELINES

=======================================================
VOICE PILLARS
=======================================================

1. DIRECT
We say what we mean. We don't bury the lead or hedge our opinions. If we believe something, we state it clearly and explain why.

Wrong: "It might be worth considering whether some meetings could potentially be reduced..."
Right: "Most meetings are optional. Here's how to tell which ones."

2. PRACTICAL
We give people things they can use today, not someday. Every piece of content should have at least one takeaway the reader can act on before their next meeting.

Wrong: "Building an async culture requires a fundamental shift in how teams communicate."
Right: "Start here: for the next 5 days, replace one recurring meeting with a written update. See what happens."

3. CONFIDENT BUT NOT ARROGANT
We have opinions and we defend them with evidence. We're not aggressive, condescending, or dismissive of alternative approaches. We're confident enough to acknowledge when something is complex.

Wrong: "If your team still has daily standups, you're doing remote work wrong."
Right: "Daily standups work for some teams. Here's a framework for knowing if they're working for yours."

4. HUMAN
We write like a smart colleague, not a consultant. We use contractions. We write short sentences. We occasionally use a dash — like this — for emphasis. We're not trying to impress; we're trying to be useful.

=======================================================
STYLE RULES
=======================================================

DO:
- Use "you" and "your team" — address the reader directly
- Start paragraphs with the key point, not context-setting
- Use specific numbers when available ("73% of remote workers" not "most remote workers")
- Use active voice ("You can reduce meetings by..." not "Meetings can be reduced by...")
- Use em-dashes for asides (not parentheses)
- Use "and" to start sentences sometimes — it's fine

DON'T:
- Use corporate buzzwords: leverage, synergy, paradigm, disrupt, ecosystem, learnings (use "lessons")
- Use "in conclusion" or "to summarize" — just conclude or summarise
- Use passive voice more than once per section
- Use more than one exclamation point per article (for any non-casual content)
- Start with "I" in LinkedIn posts — it's a known engagement killer

=======================================================
TONE BY FORMAT
=======================================================

Blog posts: confident, educational, structured. Like a smart mentor sharing hard-won lessons.

LinkedIn: personal, direct, slightly vulnerable. Share a real observation or experience. Invite discussion.

Twitter/X: punchy, concrete, slightly provocative. Each tweet should be able to stand alone.

Email newsletter: warm, personal, collegial. Write to a friend who happens to be an ops manager.

=======================================================
AUDIENCE AWARENESS
=======================================================

Primary reader: Alex (the Overloaded Ops Manager) — experienced enough to be skeptical of hype, busy enough to skim ruthlessly. Earn their attention in the first sentence.

We never punch down (laugh at bad managers) or preach (tell people what they "should" do without empathy for why it's hard).
